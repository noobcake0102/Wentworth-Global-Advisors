Case Studies  ·  LEO / MEO Satellite

Ground stations failing in the field. Working capital trapped at $76M.

A London-based public-company division designing and manufacturing ground base stations for LEO and MEO satellite constellations was a recent acquisition from its founder. The business had real customer demand and unbuildable contracts. We were brought in as interim Managing Director to recover the customer relationships, design discipline, supply chain, and cash position simultaneously.

LEO/MEO Satellite Ground Stations

UK Public Company Division · Recent Acquisition

Interim Managing Director

Multi-quarter recovery

The company designs and manufactures ground base stations for LEO and MEO satellite constellations — equipment that lives outdoors, in extreme heat and cold, in high humidity and salt air, for years. Many of the previous owner’s sold contracts had been written before he understood what those environments would do to his designs.

The situation

  • Budgeted monthly revenue of $17M. Achieving $6.5M, with large swings in both directions.
  • Margins at 8% — cash flow negative.
  • Working capital ballooned to roughly $76M, much of it trapped in finished-good WIP that customers wouldn’t accept.
  • Many fielded ground stations were failing electrically, corroding, and leaking in heavy rain.
  • Customers were waiting for replacement product to swap out failing kit in the field. Their patience was finite.

The diagnosis

The previous owner — a strong electrical engineer — had signed most of the contracts in question before selling. The contracts called for “commercial specs.” When we asked for the actual specifications used to build and test, what we received were mechanical drawings and schematics. There were no environmental performance specifications. There was no design-for-manufacturability or design-for-testability discipline. The designs were difficult to manufacture, difficult to test, difficult to install, and difficult to repair in the field.

That, combined with a stockroom drowning in inventory and a production area still organized around prototype-style batch builds, explained both the field failures and the trapped working capital.

What we did

  • Engaged customers and principals. Walked the situation with senior leadership and with each major customer. Built the working relationships needed to support a recovery commitment.
  • Wrote a real environmental specification. Engineering created an environmental specification based on MIL-STD-810, modified appropriately for commercial deployment. Corresponding test routines were developed to qualify materials, designs, and production lots against environments the equipment would actually face.
  • Installed design for manufacturability and testability. Brought IPD discipline into the engineering organization so future revisions and new programs would be born producible and testable.
  • Re-laid the production area. Transformed the floor from batch to single-piece flow, with testing in-line rather than transporting large antennas and subassemblies between cells. The result was faster cycle time, less handling damage, and a more flexible operation.
  • Workforce communication. Engaged operators and engineers directly — explained the principles, asked for input, and gave them ownership of the new flow.
  • Personnel changes. Made several deliberate changes to put experienced engineers, operations personnel, and supply-chain leaders into the organization.
  • Stockroom rationalization. Reduced stockroom inventory by 18% — about $736K — bringing it down to roughly $3.9M. Freed the working capital and reduced the noise on the floor.
  • Trapped-WIP qualification. Worked design changes into the existing finished-good WIP wherever possible to qualify and ship product, converting roughly $17M of trapped inventory into revenue and cash.
  • Field replacement program. Began replacing failing fielded equipment with units built to the new environmental and electrical requirements, restoring customer confidence in the platform.

Monthly revenue moved to $12M with normal favorable / unfavorable noise quarter to quarter, but a steadily improving trend toward the $17M target on the agreed recovery schedule. Cash flow and margins both turned favorable. Customer satisfaction shifted from outright frustration to cautious optimism. Several add-on contracts were booked, growing backlog to roughly $123M — and several expected add-ons were deliberately held back as customers continued to watch the recovery, meaning the backlog would continue to grow as performance held.

Why this matters

This engagement combined three disciplines that don’t often live in the same advisor: satellite industry operations consulting, post-acquisition integration, and supply chain consulting for aerospace companies. The recovery wasn’t a single intervention. It was a coordinated rebuild of the specification regime, the design process, the production layout, the supply chain, and the customer relationship — with cash flow improvement falling out as a consequence of getting the operating fundamentals right.

It also demonstrates the special role a fractional executive plays after a founder sale: someone who can preserve what made the founder successful, professionalize what was missing, and represent the business credibly to public-company customers without losing the engineers who built the technology in the first place.
