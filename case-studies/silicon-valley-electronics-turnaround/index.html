Case Studies  ·  Silicon Valley Electronics

$96M past-due backlog. Four customers on-site. Nine months to recover trust.

A 30-year Silicon Valley defense and satellite electronics business, recently bought by a small public company, was failing to ramp. Four customers had stationed monitoring teams on-site full time. We were brought in as interim Managing Director and rebuilt the design discipline, the stockroom, the customer relationships, and the workforce culture — in nine months.

Defense & Satellite Electronics

Recently acquired by small public co.

Interim Managing Director

9 months

For most of its history, the company had designed and produced high-frequency, high-power tubes for specialist defense and communications use — small, one-off batches for bespoke applications. Then it landed solid-state equivalents for satellite-based WiFi providers and several prime and subprime military communications programs. The contracts were transformational. The operation could not deliver them.

The situation

  • Budgeted monthly revenue of $13M. Achieving $4.5M.
  • Past-due backlog of $96M.
  • Significantly behind schedule on every contract.
  • Two new revolutionary products were failing qualification testing.
  • Four customers were paying for personnel to be on-site full-time monitoring the situation.

The diagnosis

We assessed the operation, met with the principals, and spoke directly with the key customers. Many issues; four big drivers.

  1. No design for producibility or testability. An engineer had to babysit most units out the door. Most testing was done at the box level. Designs weren’t easily assembled, disassembled, and reassembled.
  2. Contracts and specs weren’t clear or specific enough. Customers were failing units in their incoming test, requiring design changes and full requalification.
  3. No formal configuration control. The result was multiple builds at down-rev designs, with predictable rework, scrap, and customer dissatisfaction.
  4. Very poor material control and procurement. The stockroom was a graveyard of obsolete and excess material. Material loss was running above 21%.

What we did

Over a nine-month engagement as interim Managing Director, we executed a coordinated program of fixes — the kind of operational turnaround for an underperforming company that requires both top-down authority and bottom-up engineering credibility.

  • Brought in IPD experts to install Integrated Product Development for producibility and testability. Triaged the top three product lines.
  • Stockroom and material reset. Brought in a third-party partner to update stockroom locations, isolate and remove obsolete and excess material, relocate inventory to improve pull efficiency, and create a lean floor-stock setup.
  • Cleaned up specs and contracts. Worked with engineers, the contracts team, and the customers themselves to rewrite specifications and contracts with the precision needed to pass incoming test the first time.
  • Cross-site and external help. Pulled in resources from sister sites and selected outside expertise to execute three concurrent product-line redesigns.
  • Workforce engagement. Spent significant time with the workforce, who were demoralized after months of public failure. Restored a communication cadence that simply hadn’t existed.
  • Customer relationships. Pulled the four on-site customer monitoring teams into the solution rather than treating them as inspectors.

The outcome

Over nine months we rebuilt customer confidence on the selected programs. Revenue was running at $10M per month by the end of the engagement — up from $4.5M when we started. Net margin had turned favorable, and that was after taking the full write-downs for excess and obsolete material, scrap, and other trapped costs.

Material loss dropped from above 21% to below 6%, with more activity already planned. We left behind new engineering and operations staff trained in IPD with a detailed plan to review the existing product portfolio for cost trades and redesign opportunities.

Why this matters

This is a textbook business turnaround consultant engagement in defense and satellite electronics. The lever was not finding a magic process. It was design discipline, contract clarity, configuration control, material discipline, and workforce confidence, executed simultaneously by a leader the customers, the team, and ownership all trusted.
