5 months to recovery

A small-batch R&D metallurgy operation in the aerospace industry — novel-process, high-temperature components for space-based and aircraft vehicles — had just won its first volume production contract. They had six months of trial-and-error trying to recreate the “formula” that produced their one good batch. The contract was on the line.

The situation

The PE-owned R&D metallurgy company had won its first high-volume aerospace production contract: $6M, multi-part-number, integrated into the customer’s end product as matched ship sets. The challenge was that the company had spent its history producing one-off bespoke batches, and the production approach reflected it.

  • Six dedicated machines, each home-built from a common core but each subtly different in operation — the kind of variation that doesn’t matter for one-off R&D and ruins repeatable production.
  • Multiple part numbers in differing quantities had to be produced across these machines to assemble matched ship sets for the customer’s integration line.
  • Several post-process steps after the initial growth step. Validation, tooling, and fixturing had been built for one-batch work, not repetitive volume.
  • Six months of trial-and-error attempting to duplicate the original good batch had produced no repeatable result. Engineering was demoralized; the customer was on the verge of escalating.

The diagnosis

We began with the engineers, not the executive summary. What are your three most important X-input variables? The team listed temperature, electric current, and salt concentration. Then the harder question: How are you measuring those?

  • Temperature was being read from the power supply — not from inside the sealed electroplating cell where the chemistry actually happened.
  • Electric current was running through frayed wires and alligator-clip connections that varied machine to machine.
  • Salt concentration was sampled monthly and shipped to an outside lab, with one-to-two week expedited turnaround — against a 30-day cycle time.

In other words: the company was attempting to recreate a one-off batch by tuning variables it could not actually measure, on machines it could not actually compare. That was the diagnosis.

What we did

This became a textbook lean and Six Sigma intervention, executed personally on the floor over the recovery period.

  • Direct measurement, in-cell. Engineers drilled a port in the lid, installed a crystal tube down into the solution, and ran thermal sensors continuously through the 30-day cycle.
  • Repeatable electrical input. All wiring replaced and alligator-clip connections hard-wired so each machine saw the same current under the same conditions.
  • Salt concentration in real time. Brought salt-bath testing in-house, so concentration was checked between every run instead of monthly with a two-week lag.
  • Balanced production scheduling. Built a pull-system schedule with algorithms to balance multiple part numbers across the six growth machines and the seven shared post-processing steps.
  • TPM and lean fundamentals. Total Productive Maintenance, SMED to enable multi-part-number runs, and the lean-flow disciplines that allow a single line to handle quality and on-time delivery for varying quantities of multiple part numbers.
  • SPC on the X-input variables. Once measurable, they were placed under statistical process control so the team would see drift before it generated nonconforming product.
  • Customer relationship recovery. Personally engaged the customer and presented a recovery commitment we could honor.

We created a production line where all six machines produced like product by reducing variability between — and within — each. Within five months the original $6M contract was on its agreed recovery schedule. During those final months, customer confidence had returned to the point that they added new contracts for the same part numbers totaling $12M, and began qualification on three additional part-number sets potentially worth another $8M.

Two years later, the company had added a dedicated building for this product line and was running at $5M per month with continued growth. They had also added a second customer — a significant subprime in the aerospace industry — on the strength of the production system we built.

Why this matters

This engagement is a working illustration of how to scale production in aerospace manufacturing when the company’s operating DNA is R&D rather than volume production. The fix was not exotic. It was the patient application of lean and Six Sigma fundamentals — lean six sigma implementation consulting in its purest form — combined with the operational leadership willing to walk the floor and ask engineers the right questions.
